Question One
What is one weakness you have & what steps have you taken to overcome it?

I say yes to everything.
That used to be a problem.

When I moved from Indonesia to Japan at 18, I learned that every opportunity might be the only one. So I said yes — to every project, every side initiative, every interesting problem. I shipped everything I committed to. But the quality of my presence suffered — I was in every room, but rarely fully in any of them.

The turning point was my capstone at MINDS Singapore. I'd taken it on alongside a full-time job, a Master's programme, and SEA-Up. Halfway through, I realised I was giving the people who needed me most — staff and participants at a disability organisation — the smallest share of my attention.

I didn't stop being curious. But I built a filter: does this serve the people I've committed to, or does it just serve my curiosity?

SEA-Up is the result of that discipline. One venture. Full focus. 10 weeks this summer.

Question Two
Describe the single most impressive thing that you have built, created, or achieved outside of your company.

Six months at MINDS changed what I thought I was building.

In mid-2025, I started a capstone at MINDS Singapore — the region's largest organisation serving people with intellectual disabilities. The brief: explore how AI could help their staff. What followed was six months of juggling a full-time job, a Master's programme, and fieldwork — all while trying to do justice to a community that deserved more than a half-hearted effort.

The paper was IRB-approved and peer-reviewed. But the most important finding wasn't about efficiency — it was what I called the "Augmentation Limit": AI multiplies existing human competence, it doesn't substitute for it.

If AI can't substitute for human capability, then the tools need redesigning — built around how people with intellectual disabilities actually think and communicate. That's when SEA-Up became a platform.

Question Three
Describe what integrity means to you.

Integrity is what you build when no one is checking.

When you build for people with intellectual disabilities, every design decision carries moral weight. They may never audit your code or negotiate their terms. That asymmetry doesn't reduce responsibility — it multiplies it.

IP belongs to the creator

Enforced at the database level — architecture, not just policy.

Honest scope boundaries

A mock marketplace is labelled as a mock. Nothing is overpromised.

Two-layer safety

AI moderation + human facilitator review. Neither layer is optional.

Name the weaknesses

Cold-start, facilitator dependency — documented honestly, not hidden.

I'm not a disability services expert. I'm a technologist who spent six months listening to people who are. The research led me here — not the assumption that I already had the answers.

Build as if every person you serve will one day see every decision you made when they weren't in the room — and be certain they'd still trust you.
